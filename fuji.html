Mount Fuji

Mount Fuji, Japan’s highest peak, is an iconic symbol of natural beauty and cultural significance. Discover the mountain's unique features, its importance in Japanese culture, and tips for capturing the best views

About the Mountain

Standing at 3,776 meters (12,389 feet), Mount Fuji is a stratovolcano located on Honshu Island, straddling Shizuoka and Yamanashi Prefectures. Its nearly symmetrical cone shape makes it a striking landmark, visible from Tokyo on clear days. Revered as a sacred site in Shinto and Buddhist traditions, Mount Fuji has been a destination for pilgrims and artists throughout history.

The Significance of Mount Fuji in Japan

Mount Fuji holds a special place in Japanese culture and spirituality. Revered as a sacred site, it has been a destination for pilgrims and artists for centuries. The mountain is deeply linked to Shinto beliefs, with many shrines dedicated to it, and it is also a prominent subject in Japanese art and literature.

How to Get the Best View

Capturing the perfect view of Mount Fuji requires planning and timing. Here’s how to make the most of your experience:

  • Viewing Spots: The Five Lakes (Fujigoko) area offers excellent panoramic views, with Lake Kawaguchi being particularly popular. For a city view, the Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building's observation decks offer distant but impressive views of the mountain.


  • Best Times: Early morning and late afternoon provide the clearest and most dramatic views, with sunrise or sunset offering particularly stunning photo opportunities. Winter months often provide the clearest skies, but summer climbers should also consider the vibrant backdrop of surrounding greenery.


  • Climbing Season: If you’re interested in climbing Mount Fuji, the official climbing season is from early July to early September. During this period, facilities and guides are available, and the weather conditions are more favorable.
